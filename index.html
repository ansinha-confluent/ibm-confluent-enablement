Overview

Confluent × IBM: how it all actually fits together

IBM closed the Confluent acquisition on 17 March 2026 (~$11B, $31/share). I wrote this for the engineers who have to actually understand what changed, whether you're selling it, deploying it, or building on top of it. So this is the engineer-to-engineer version: where the two stacks really bolt together, which configs you'll actually type, and the places the integration still makes you sweat. No pitch. Just the wiring.

Here's the good news if you already live in Kafka and Flink: you don't have to relearn much. Confluent is still Confluent, the real-time substrate underneath everything. What's new is the stuff around it. You get a governed lakehouse (watsonx.data), a reasoning and agent layer on top (watsonx.ai + Orchestrate), governance and lineage that reach across the whole thing, and proper paths off the mainframe. IBM keeps describing the deal as additive, and I think that's the honest read: "connect, process and govern real-time data for applications and AI agents."

Why you should care

This isn't a logo on a slide. IBM is wiring Confluent straight into watsonx, into hybrid integration, into Z. The streaming layer is the center of their real-time AI story, not a bolt-on. So when a customer asks where this is going, the integration points in this guide are the answer. Learn them.

The three-layer model (learn this one mental picture)

If you take one thing from this guide, take this. Nearly every IBM + Confluent architecture you'll ever whiteboard collapses into three layers and a governance spine running down the side. Once that picture is in your head, the pillar pages stop looking like four unrelated products and start looking like one stack.

  1. Nervous system (Confluent). Kafka is the durable event log, Flink does the sub-second processing, and Connectors plus Tableflow move data in and out. Think of this as the layer where "now" lives.
  2. Data foundation (IBM watsonx.data). An open lakehouse: Iceberg and Parquet tables, queried by Presto (C++/Velox) and Spark, with Milvus sitting alongside for vectors. This is where "everything, organized and governed" lives.
  3. Reason and act (watsonx.ai + watsonx Orchestrate). Granite models and RAG do the thinking. Orchestrate runs the multi-step agent workflows, with a human in the loop and MCP tool calls when you need them.

Running down the side of all three is the governance spine: watsonx.governance plus watsonx.data intelligence for lineage. Here's the point people miss. Governing the lake but not the streams that feed it is governing half the system. The spine has to reach back into the event layer, or it isn't really governance.

When to use what

The question every customer eventually asks is "okay, but which layer owns this workload?" The most useful thing you can hand them is a clean rule for answering that without a two-hour debate. This is the one I keep coming back to, and it holds up:

If the workload is…It belongs to…Why
Sub-minute real-time context, stream processing, event distributionConfluent / FlinkLatency and replay are first-class; Flink does stateful processing in motion
Multi-step business workflow, human-in-the-loop, tool/agent orchestrationwatsonx OrchestrateDurable, supervised, long-running, not a stream job
Historical / at-rest analytics, federated SQL, AI training datawatsonx.data lakehouseOpen tables, cost-efficient engines, governed once

In practice almost every real design uses all three, and the fun part, the part where you earn your keep, is the handoffs between them. That's the whole subject of the watsonx.data page, because Tableflow is the handoff from layer 1 to layer 2.

Shared open standards mean less lock-in

The reason these handoffs are cheap is that both companies already bet on the same open formats: Apache Kafka, Apache Flink, Apache Iceberg, Apache Parquet, Presto, and MCP (Model Context Protocol) for wiring up tools and agents. So when a customer gets nervous about lock-in, you have a straight answer for them. The streaming engine and the table format are open standards. What you're paying IBM and Confluent for is the managed surface around them, not a cage you can't leave.

What's GA vs. preview (please be precise about this)

This is the line that gets people in trouble. Mix up shipped and roadmap in front of a customer and you'll spend the next call walking it back, so keep these two buckets straight.

  • Shipping today (GA): the Confluent, Tableflow, and Flink integrations with watsonx.data (announced at Think on 5 May 2026), the IBM MQ connectors, Confluent Platform certified on Linux on Z / s390x, and IBM Confluent Platform as a product (standalone, or as a CP4I / webMethods add-on).
  • Roadmap, not shipped yet: "Context in watsonx.data" (the federated semantic layer), the next-gen multi-agent Orchestrate control plane, GPU-accelerated Presto, the native watsonx.data Sink connector, and the Real-Time Context Engine. If you mention any of these, say "roadmap" in the same breath.
Accuracy note carried into the Integration page

Watch this one. There is no public formal Statement of Direction deprecating IBM Event Streams or Event Processing. IBM still ships and updates both. So the line is "IBM Confluent Platform is the strategic go-forward streaming engine and the natural consolidation target," never "Event Streams is EOL." The Integration page digs into why that distinction matters.

How to use this guide

There are three pillar pages, and each one stands on its own, so jump to whatever the customer in front of you cares about. watsonx.data is the data-at-rest handoff. IBM Integration covers MQ, Event Automation, webMethods, and StreamSets. IBM Z is the mainframe CDC and hybrid story. Every page opens with an architecture diagram, gets into the components, configs, and the gotchas that bite, then walks two or three real use cases with their own diagrams, and finishes with the references so you can check my work.

Pillar 1

Confluent + IBM watsonx.data

The short version: watsonx.data is an open lakehouse. Iceberg and Parquet tables, queried by Presto (C++/Velox) and Spark, running on IBM Cloud, AWS, Azure, GCP, or on-prem (OpenShift, IBM Storage Fusion). The thing that connects it to Confluent is Tableflow, and this is the part worth understanding: it keeps a Kafka topic continuously materialized as an Iceberg table, zero-copy, so you never have to stand up and then babysit a second ETL pipeline just to get streaming data into the lake. Where it gets interesting is the catalog wiring, one hard read-only rule that surprises people, and the exact point where the managed-storage convenience runs out.

How Tableflow actually works

Picture Tableflow as a process that watches a Kafka topic and keeps an Iceberg (or Delta) table in lockstep with it. It pulls the topic's schema from Schema Registry, turns Avro, JSON Schema, or Protobuf records into Parquet, and quietly handles the annoying parts: automated schema evolution (adding and dropping columns, widening types), CDC materialization, and Parquet compaction so you don't end up with the classic swamp of a million tiny files. You can use the built-in Iceberg REST Catalog, or sync the tables out to an external one like AWS Glue, Snowflake Open Catalog (Polaris), or Databricks Unity. For storage you pick either Confluent's managed storage or BYOS (bring your own, on S3 or IBM COS).

Tableflow scope limits

Tableflow is Confluent Cloud only right now. Not Confluent Platform, and not GCP-backed clusters at GA. Snapshot retention only goes from 10 to 100 snapshots, nothing outside that band. If the customer walks in assuming "Tableflow everywhere, including my on-prem CP cluster," correct that on the first call before it becomes a design assumption. (On-prem materialization is starting to show up via WarpStream Tableflow plus watsonx.data on-prem, but treat it as roadmap for now.)

Wiring watsonx.data to Tableflow: the exact config

What you're doing here is registering the Tableflow Iceberg REST catalog as a data source inside watsonx.data. There are two engines you can point at it, Spark and Presto, and each has its own config plus its own little set of traps. Here's both.

Spark engine

spark.sql.catalog.tableflow            = org.apache.iceberg.spark.SparkCatalog
spark.sql.catalog.tableflow.type       = rest
spark.sql.catalog.tableflow.uri        = https://tableflow.{REGION}.aws.confluent.cloud/iceberg/catalog/organizations/{ORG_ID}/environments/{ENV_ID}
spark.sql.catalog.tableflow.credential = {APIKEY}:{SECRET}
spark.sql.catalog.tableflow.io-impl    = org.apache.iceberg.aws.s3.S3FileIO
spark.sql.catalog.tableflow.s3.remote-signing-enabled = true   # required for managed storage
# namespace = your Kafka cluster id (lkc-xxxxx); table name = the topic name

The uri is just the Tableflow REST endpoint scoped to your org and environment, and the credential is a Cloud API key and secret pair. The line that does the clever bit is s3.remote-signing-enabled=true: with managed storage, that's what lets Spark read the data files without you ever handing it raw bucket credentials. Confluent signs the requests on Spark's behalf.

Presto engine

connector.name         = iceberg
iceberg.catalog.type   = REST
iceberg.rest.uri       = <tableflow catalog uri>
iceberg.rest.auth.type = OAUTH2
Presto gotchas (the ones that bite)

Three things that will trip you up. First, Presto does not support Confluent managed storage or vended credentials, so with Presto you have no choice, it's BYOS. Second, Presto won't auto-discover Tableflow tables, so you have to name exact topics and tables. Third, the namespace is the cluster id (lkc-xxxxx), which has hyphens in it, so quote it in SQL or it'll blow up. Provision the whole thing as a custom Iceberg data source in watsonx.data Infrastructure Manager.

The read-only constraint: design around it

Verbatim from Confluent docs

"Iceberg tables exposed via external catalog synchronization are read-only. When working with Iceberg tables created by Tableflow, it's essential to ensure that they are consumed as read-only tables."

Read that literally. No INSERT, UPDATE, or DELETE against these tables from Spark or Presto. The only write path is the stream. You change the data by publishing to the source Kafka topic, and Tableflow reflects the change downstream. I actually think this is the right model, because the log stays the single source of truth, but it reliably catches people who are used to treating a lakehouse table as something they can mutate. One more thing to file away: external catalog sync (Glue, Polaris, Unity) needs BYOS and won't work with managed storage.

Land it clean: medallion + Flink AI functions

Resist the temptation to dump raw bronze into the lake and promise to clean it up later. You won't, and now it's everyone's problem. Do the filtering, deduping, and enrichment in Flink before Tableflow, so the data lands already at silver or gold. And Confluent Cloud Flink gives you a genuinely useful set of in-SQL AI and ML functions you can call inline during that shaping step:

  • Classical ML: ML_PREDICT, ML_FORECAST, ML_DETECT_ANOMALIES (ARIMA), ML_DETECT_ANOMALIES_ROBUST (MAD).
  • Generative / foundation-model: AI_COMPLETE, AI_EMBEDDING, AI_SENTIMENT, AI_DETECT_PII, AI_DETECT_ANOMALIES (TimesFM), AI_RUN_AGENT, AI_TOOL_INVOKE.

Vectors, lineage, reasoning

  • Milvus in watsonx.data: managed vector store (the watsonx-data reference cites up to ~100M vectors × 384 dims). Run it as a parallel sink alongside Iceberg: unstructured text → embeddings → Milvus for semantic search / RAG, wired into watsonx Orchestrate.
  • watsonx.data intelligence (= IBM Data Lineage, integrating Manta) does OpenLineage-based harvesting across 50+ integrations, column-level end-to-end lineage, AI factsheets, quality and governance.
  • watsonx.ai: Granite models do the reasoning, for example pulling structured codes out of free-text notes (see the healthcare use case).
  • MCP: watsonx.data 2.2.x ships a remote MCP server; import it into Orchestrate with orchestrate toolkits import --kind mcp.
Honest hedge

IBM docs name OpenLineage and "50+ integrations," but I dug for a documented "harvests Confluent Schema Registry" path and couldn't confirm one word for word. So phrase it as "harvests operational metadata from streaming sources, including schemas." Don't claim a named Schema-Registry lineage connector you can't point at in the docs.

Use cases

★ UC-A: Real-time insurance claims fraud (catastrophe surge) flagship

Think about what actually happens after a hurricane. Claims spike, and fraud peaks at the exact moment your adjusters are drowning. Nightly batch scoring is useless here, because by the time the batch runs the damage is done. You need suspect claims flagged as they land. So: IBM MQ carries the claims, the Confluent IBM MQ Source connector drops them onto a Kafka claims topic, and Flink scores them inline with ML_DETECT_ANOMALIES or AI_DETECT_ANOMALIES. The enriched stream rides Tableflow into Iceberg, watsonx.data Presto answers the at-rest questions (approvals and denials by city, a six-month fraud look-back), and watsonx Orchestrate agents work the flagged queue.

Components: IBM MQ Source connector, Confluent Cloud Flink (the anomaly functions), Tableflow (BYOS on S3 or COS), watsonx.data Presto, and watsonx Orchestrate. One honesty note so nobody gets caught out: the Flink functions are real and cited, but the specific demo writeup isn't public. Present this as a realistic reference architecture you could build, not as a shipped IBM demo.

★ UC-B: Customer 360 with live sentiment (federated join) flagship

Clickstream and session events flow into Kafka, Flink sessionizes them and scores sentiment with AI_SENTIMENT, and Tableflow lands a live session table in Iceberg. Now watsonx.data Presto does the one thing lakehouses are genuinely great at: a federated join of that fresh session table against the historical demographics already sitting in the lake.

SELECT s.session_id, s.sentiment_score, d.segment, d.lifetime_value
FROM   tableflow."lkc-xxxxx".sessions_live  s
JOIN   hive.demographics.customer_360         d
  ON   s.customer_id = d.customer_id
WHERE  s.sentiment_score < 0.3;   -- unhappy, high-value sessions, right now

The win is no second copy and no reverse-ETL: the streaming table and the historical table sit in the same query engine. Mind the cluster-id namespace quoting (the gotcha above).

UC-C: Healthcare clinical-code extraction (governance vehicle)

Clinical events stream to Kafka → Tableflow → Iceberg → watsonx.data, where a Granite model on watsonx.ai extracts ICD-10 codes from free-text notes and reconciles them against billing. The point of this one is governance: it exercises end-to-end column-level lineage via watsonx.data intelligence and makes the read-only projection constraint concrete (the lake table is the governed read model; corrections go back through the stream).

Design considerations & gotchas (quick reference)

Before you commit a design

• Tableflow = Cloud-only, not CP, not GCP clusters (GA).
• Presto ⇒ BYOS mandatory; no managed-storage vended creds; no table auto-discovery; quote the lkc- namespace.
• Tables are read-only; the write path is the topic.
• External catalog sync (Glue/Polaris/Unity) needs BYOS.
• Shape data with Flink to land silver/gold; snapshot retention is 10 to 100.
• Schema-Registry-as-lineage-source is a hedge, not a documented connector, so phrase accordingly.

Pillar 2

Confluent + IBM Integration

The short version: IBM now ships IBM Confluent Platform as a product, both standalone and as an add-on to Cloud Pak for Integration (CP4I) and webMethods. The thing to keep in your head is that Confluent doesn't land in a vacuum. It lands in the middle of IBM's existing hybrid-integration estate: Event Automation (Event Streams / Event Processing / Event Endpoint Management), IBM MQ, App Connect, and IBM Data Integration (StreamSets, DataStage, Data Replication, Databand). Everything that follows on this page is about where Confluent slots into that estate, the MQ connector trade-offs you'll genuinely end up arguing about, and one correction that's worth getting exactly right.

The correction (say this accurately)

There is no public formal Statement of Direction deprecating Event Streams or Event Processing. As of mid-2026 IBM still markets and actively develops them (Event Streams shipped Kafka 3.8 and CLI updates in Apr 2026). What is deprecated: "Event Streams for IBM Cloud Satellite," and the CP4I-7.0.x Confluent packaging hit EOL in Apr 2025. Correct framing: IBM Confluent Platform is the strategic go-forward streaming engine and the natural consolidation target; EEM / AsyncAPI is the governance layer that carries forward. Do not quote a deprecation date. There isn't one, and the moment someone invents one in front of a customer, they've lost the room.

The Event Automation layer (on top of Confluent Kafka)

Here's the mental model I use. Event Automation is three pieces, and they don't all have the same future. Two of them are durable, one of them is the role that's slowly being handed over to Confluent.

  • Event Streams: IBM's own Kafka distribution. This is the role the consolidation story moves toward IBM Confluent Platform over time. It's alive and supported today, so don't write it off in front of a customer who's running it, but understand which way the arrow points.
  • Event Processing: a drag-and-drop, Flink-style low-code authoring UI. This is the no-code surface IBM layers on top for building stream processing without writing SQL or Java.
  • Event Endpoint Management (EEM): an AsyncAPI catalog plus a socialization and governance gateway. This is the piece that carries forward no matter which Kafka engine ends up underneath, so it's the safest part of the story to lean on.

If you need proof it scales, here are the public references worth quoting: PVcomBank at roughly 5M notifications a day, Ajman at about 50K TPS, Norsk Tipping at 5K+ gaming TPS.

IBM MQ and Confluent: coexistence and honest connector trade-offs

This is the conversation you'll have more than any other, so it's worth getting your framing straight. MQ is mission-critical transactional messaging and it isn't going anywhere. Confluent adds the things MQ was never built for: replay, fan-out to many independent consumers, stream processing, and event distribution at scale. They coexist, and the choice between them is use-case-led, not a religious war. The interesting detail lives in the connectors:

ConnectorVersion / packagingNotes
IBM OSS MQ Source / Sinksource v2.8.1, sink v2.1.2 (Apache-2.0)CLIENT or BINDINGS mode; exactly-once on Kafka 3.3+ via a dedicated state queue (tasks.max=1); XML record builder; MQMD access
Confluent Cloud fully-managed IBM MQ Sinkmanagedport 1414 default; CSFLE; RFH2 suppression (mq.destination.suppress.rfh2); exactly-once via dedicated offsets queue (EXCLUSIVE, single task); base64 TLS keystore
Confluent Platform self-managed MQ Source/Sinkself-managed; s390x from CP 8.2+HA via mq.connection.list; BINDINGS via JNI

A minimal self-managed IBM MQ Source connector looks like this:

"connector.class": "io.confluent.connect.ibm.mq.IbmMQSourceConnector",
"kafka.topic":     "claims.in",
"mq.queue.manager": "QM1",
"mq.connection.mode": "BINDINGS",        // or CLIENT (TCP/IP)
"mq.queue":         "CLAIMS.REQUEST",
"tasks.max":        "1"                  // required for exactly-once
Opinionated trade-off

My rule of thumb: reach for Confluent when the deployment is cloud or elastic, when you want fully-managed connectors, and when Schema Registry plus CSFLE are part of the ask. Reach for the IBM native connectors when you need z/OS bindings mode, when there's deep MQ-specific configuration in play, or when the customer specifically wants IBM-supported Kubernetes operator artifacts. On z/OS the answer is almost always the certified IBM MQ connectors in bindings mode, and the IBM Z page goes into why.

webMethods, App Connect, and IBM Data Integration next to Confluent DSP

There's a cluster of IBM integration products that sit near Confluent and sometimes look like they overlap. Here's what each one actually is, so you can tell them apart on a whiteboard:

  • webMethods Hybrid Integration: IBM's iPaaS, with an Event Automation component baked in. Think of it as the SaaS complement to CP4I.
  • App Connect Enterprise (ACE) 13.x: 200+ connectors, and the part worth remembering is that it can expose its flows and connectors as MCP tools. That's what makes it relevant to the agent layer.
  • IBM Data Integration: a family. StreamSets is the JVM visual low-code pipeline tool (origins → processors → destinations, with data-drift auto-adapt, and it's embedded in watsonx.data integration). DataStage is the batch ETL/ELT workhorse. Data Replication / IIDR does CDC from Db2 z/OS, VSAM, and IMS into Kafka. Databand handles observability.
Where the line falls

Reach for IBM Data Integration when the job is batch ETL, heterogeneous database movement, or pipelines that have to tolerate schema drift. Reach for Confluent DSP (Kafka commit log + Flink + Tableflow + Connect + governance) when you need sub-minute event streaming, replay, or event-native processing. They genuinely overlap in the middle, and the question that settles it every time is simple: is the unit of work an event, or a batch?

Connectors and distribution

A few connector specifics that come up often enough to be worth memorizing:

  • Db2 Source (JDBC) for LUW: fully-managed and GA on Confluent Cloud. Snapshot plus incrementing/timestamp modes, Avro/JSON_SR/Protobuf/JSON, and CSFLE.
  • Oracle CDC (XStream): a premium connector, available on both cloud and platform.
  • SAP S/4HANA: this one comes in two phases, and the distinction matters. Phase 1 is OData v4 polling and it's fully-managed. Phase 2 is true push, via ABAP partner add-ons feeding an HTTP/Webhook Source. ASAPIO is the one I'd actually commit to in writing (REST Proxy plus Webhook, Confluent-certified since Apr 2021). SNP and Onibex get named in the materials but I couldn't separately verify them, so don't oversell them.
  • Distribution: Confluent runs on-prem via IBM Passport Advantage Online. One trap to flag: Confluent's public Docker image reference lists amd64 and arm64 only. The s390x builds come through IBM distribution, not that public reference. CP is broadly certified on Linux on Z, and the IBM Z page covers it.

Use cases

★ UC-A: Event Streams / CP4I → IBM Confluent Platform consolidation flagship

Picture the before and after. Before: CP4I-embedded Event Streams (Kafka) plus Event Processing plus EEM. After: IBM Confluent Platform as the streaming engine, with EEM/AsyncAPI carried forward as the governance layer and Event Processing's low-code surface still sitting on top. The framing that keeps you honest: this is the steered consolidation path, the direction IBM is pointing customers, and it is emphatically not a forced EOL migration with a deadline stapled to it.

★ UC-B: webMethods → Confluent Cloud → watsonx.data (data availability) flagship

The flow is short and clean: webMethods publishes to a Confluent Cloud topic, an optional light Flink step shapes it, Tableflow lands it in Iceberg, and it shows up in watsonx.data. Be clear about what you're optimizing for. The goal is data-availability latency, getting webMethods-integrated data into the lakehouse fast, not winning a query benchmark. It's the cleanest way I know to make iPaaS-sourced data analytics-ready without hand-rolling a bespoke pipeline.

UC-C: MQ modernization / event distribution at scale

You don't rip out MQ. You keep it as the transactional backbone, bridge it to Confluent with the MQ Source/Sink connectors, and then let downstream microservices, analytics in watsonx.data, and AI agents all consume the same governed streams, with the replay and fan-out that MQ on its own was never going to give you. Just be straight about the connector trade-offs from the table above: bindings mode and MQ-specific config push you toward the native connectors, while managed plus Schema Registry pushes you toward Confluent's.

Pillar 3

Confluent + IBM Z

The short version: IBM Z is the z/Architecture mainframe, and its Linux architecture is s390x (64-bit), running z/OS, Linux on Z, or LinuxONE. The one piece of jargon you have to internalize before any of this makes sense is the zIIP (Z Integrated Information Processor). It's a specialty engine for eligible Java workloads, and the magic is that its capacity doesn't count toward MIPS and isn't chargeable under workload-based software maintenance. So when you push Confluent's Java connectors onto zIIP, you're moving real-time mainframe integration off the general-purpose engines the customer is actually billed on. That's the whole game. And before anyone dismisses the mainframe as legacy: north of 70% of the Fortune 500 and around 92% of the top-100 banks still run them, so this is where a lot of the money quietly lives.

Connect on z/OS: the verified specifics

Here's something a lot of people don't realize: Confluent certifies Kafka Connect to run on z/OS itself, hosting the certified premium IBM MQ Source v12.1.1 and Sink v2.1.2. Once you know that's possible, the detail that decides your cost and security posture is the connection mode, and this is the part people get wrong:

Run it in BINDINGS mode

On z/OS, run the MQ connectors in BINDINGS mode (JNI, in-memory, local to the MQ address space, via MQCNO_STANDARD_BINDINGS). This avoids routing through the CHINIT address spaces that CLIENT (TCP/IP) mode requires, so it's more secure and uses far less networking and CHINIT capacity. CLIENT mode is the fallback when Connect runs off-platform.

And a handful of z/OS specifics that will absolutely bite you during setup if nobody warned you: it's EBCDIC IBM-1047 via iconv, it runs under USS plus Bash, the Java workload is zIIP-eligible (the whole point), the Log4j2 charset has to be set to IBM-1047, JCL redirects STDOUT/STDERR to USS files, it's certified on z/OS v2.4, and it needs com.ibm.mq.allclient.jar. Use v12.x for at-least-once delivery; v11.x is deprecated, so don't start a new build on it.

Value and outcomes: label internal vs. public

Be disciplined about which numbers are published

Public (Confluent blog / MIPS whitepaper): zIIP offload reduces processing cost; the pattern vastly cuts MQ networking + CHINIT cost; named customers Alight, RBC, KeyBank. Internal pilot figures (present as such, not as published doc claims): ~10× cheaper / ~10× faster; ~30K msg/s at ~3KB with ~$1.2M projected savings; another engagement cut MQ MIPS by ~90%. If you put the internal numbers on a slide, label them "internal pilot," and cite the public blog for the directional claim.

Mainframe sources and CDC options

The sources you'll be pulling from are the usual mainframe suspects: Db2 for z/OS, VSAM, IMS, CICS/COBOL, and IBM MQ. Getting data out of them is easy to wave your hands at and hard to do well, and the CDC choice is where the real architecture decision lives. Here's how I'd weigh the options:

  • IIDR (IBM Data Replication): reads Db2 active logs; the canonical IBM path to stream z/OS Db2 → Kafka; also handles VSAM/IMS. Cases: Garanti BBVA, a Middle East bank VSAM CIF. Carries licensing cost.
  • IBM Data Gate for Confluent: log-based CDC from Db2 z/OS to Confluent, emitting Debezium-compatible events, 96% zIIP-eligible, with initial load + continuous CDC, Avro/JSON + Schema Registry + TLS. Publicly announced 2026-05-05; present as "announced May 2026, GA mid-2026" (don't over-pin the GA date).
  • zDIH (IBM Z Digital Integration Hub): an in-memory cache of z/OS systems-of-record that also publishes events to Kafka; shields production from inquiry traffic; zIIP-optimized. Case: M&T Bank.
  • CICS/COBOL pattern: CICS → CICS-MQ adapter → IBM MQ → self-managed IBM MQ Source connector on z/OS → Kafka.
  • Third-party CDC (mention only, where IBM tooling isn't licensed): Precisely, Oracle GoldenGate, Informatica, Qlik, tcVision.
The honest gap

There is no fully-managed Confluent connector for Db2 z/OS yet. Today the real options are IIDR (licensed) or IBM Data Gate (newly announced). Don't imply a one-click managed Db2-z/OS source exists.

Confluent Platform on s390x / LinuxONE and hybrid

You can also run Confluent itself on the box. IBM support states that "IBM Confluent Platform for Z and LinuxONE is broadly certified to run on Linux on Z (s390x)," covering the majority of features, with multi-arch Docker images on OpenShift/Kubernetes via CFK (Confluent for Kubernetes). Your deployment targets are LinuxONE, Linux on Z, or zCX (which itself runs on an IFL or zIIP).

Hedge on the component list

The full per-component certification list (ksqlDB / Control Center / REST Proxy / Flink) isn't individually enumerated publicly, so say "core CP components," not a specific checklist, unless you've confirmed it for the customer's version.

The hybrid topology is the one I'd actually push. Run core streaming on Z or LinuxONE, then use Cluster Linking to mirror topics out to Confluent Cloud, byte-for-byte and offset-preserving, from on-prem CP 7.0+ into Cloud. You get the best of both: low-latency mainframe-adjacent processing stays on-platform, while the cloud side feeds watsonx.data and all your elastic consumers. When the conversation turns to consolidation economics, the citable source is the Signal65/IBM LinuxONE TCO work (for example, 47 x86 cores collapsing to 11 IFLs).

Don't overstate the case studies

The Fiserv LinuxONE-specific Confluent case is not citable; Fiserv's Signature modernization is on Power10, not LinuxONE. Use Signal65 for LinuxONE consolidation numbers and the Confluent zSystems blog (Alight/RBC/KeyBank) for the streaming story.

Use cases

★ UC-A: Mainframe MQ offload for cost + real-time access flagship

The shape is straightforward and it's the one that pays for itself fastest. A CICS application puts messages on MQ on z/OS, and the certified IBM MQ Source connector, running on zIIP in bindings mode, lands them on Kafka off the mainframe, where cloud apps and analytics pick them up. For outcomes you've got the public zIIP cost-reduction story, plus the clearly-labeled internal pilots (around 30K msg/s, roughly $1.2M savings, about 90% MIPS reduction) and named customers RBC, Alight, and KeyBank. Keep the internal numbers tagged as internal when you present them.

★ UC-B: Db2 z/OS CDC for real-time analytics/AI flagship

This is the pattern that turns the mainframe system of record into a real-time feed without bolting extra query load onto Db2, which is the thing your DBAs are terrified of. You stream Db2 for z/OS active logs through IIDR or IBM Data Gate (Debezium-style, 96% zIIP) into Confluent, then Tableflow into watsonx.data for at-rest analytics and AI. Two honest caveats to say out loud: there's no fully-managed Db2-z/OS connector yet, and IIDR carries a licensing cost. Don't let either one surprise the customer later.

UC-C: Confluent Platform on LinuxONE consolidation + hybrid

The full chain reads: mainframe sources → CDC → Confluent Platform running on LinuxONE (s390x, CFK on OpenShift) → Cluster Linking → Confluent Cloud → watsonx.data. The pitch writes itself: you consolidate sprawling x86 streaming infrastructure down onto IFLs (lean on the Signal65 TCO numbers here) while keeping a clean, offset-preserving bridge to the cloud for the AI and analytics side. You're not choosing between mainframe and cloud, you're using each for what it's best at.

Design considerations & gotchas (quick reference)

Before you commit a design

• Use BINDINGS mode on z/OS (zIIP-eligible, avoids CHINIT/TCP overhead); CLIENT only off-platform.
• MQ Source v12.1.1 / Sink v2.1.2; v12.x at-least-once; v11.x deprecated; EBCDIC IBM-1047, USS+Bash, z/OS v2.4.
• No fully-managed Db2-z/OS connector; it's IIDR (licensed) or IBM Data Gate (announced May 2026).
• CP on s390x = "core components," not a confirmed full feature list; via IBM distribution + CFK.
• Cluster Linking is byte-for-byte/offset-preserving from CP 7.0+ → Cloud.
• Label internal pilot numbers as such; Fiserv LinuxONE case is not citable.
